## Draft (spoken) — flow v2

> **[SLIDE: Sisyphus — boulder mid-roll, *downhill*. He's below it, hands up, holding it back.]**
>
> We're told to imagine Sisyphus happy. I'd like to imagine him *accurately* first — because Camus got one thing wrong. The direction. The boulder doesn't go up. It rolls **down** — and Sisyphus goes down *with* it.
>
> And that motion — find the slope, go down, minimize something — is everything interesting in this room. A neural network calls it gradient descent. A galaxy survey calls it fitting a model. And your brain has run the same loop your whole life, minimizing surprise one prediction at a time. **The most fashionable idea in AI is the oldest idea in your skull.**
>
> But you can't descend until you answer two questions nobody hands you. What are you minimizing — what's the *loss*? And underneath it: what does "close" even *mean* — close in miles, in redshift, in how well I know you? Every loss is built on a distance — and **choosing that distance is itself an optimization.** You have to go down just to decide which way *is* down. Loops inside loops.
>
> So you pick a distance, you build a loss, you descend — and the moment you reach the bottom, the target moves. The landscape lifts under your feet.
>
> That's not the curse. That's the **point.** A slope that stops moving has no gradient — and no gradient means nothing left to do. Hold that thought.
